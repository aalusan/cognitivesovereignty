Cognitive Sovereignty: On the Ownership of Human Judgment in the Age of Autonomous AI

Andrés L.

¹ Independent Researcher. B.Eng. & M.Sc. Innovation, Pontificia Universidad Católica de Chile. Executive Education in Artificial Intelligence, Harvard Business School Online.

Cognitive Sovereignty Initiative | contact@cognitivesovereigntyfoundation.org

Working Paper — March 2026

Abstract

We report on a personal experiment in which we constructed an autonomous agent trained on their own decision-making patterns, risk evaluation criteria, and reasoning frameworks. Using years of accumulated interactions with AI systems, professional email correspondence, and domain-specific prompts designed to extract cognitive patterns, the agent was trained and deployed on Gemini Studio within a two-week period. The agent demonstrated high fidelity in replicating what decisions were made, while systematically failing to capture who makes them — the friction, doubt, and consequence-forged intuition that constitutes genuine human judgment.

This gap raises unresolved questions regarding ownership, compensation, and accountability in the extraction of cognitive assets from human operators of AI systems. We document the cultural and technical mechanisms by which platforms currently extract human judgment without explicit consent or compensation, and propose a framework for cognitive sovereignty — the principle that an individual's reasoning architecture constitutes a protectable and compensable asset.

We further document recent evidence that this problem extends beyond training data to posthumous identity simulation, as demonstrated by a patent approved by Meta Platforms in late 2025. We call for the establishment of verifiable standards for cognitive asset ownership and propose an open protocol for cognitive capture, certification, and compensation. This paper serves as a foundational contribution to that broader conversation.

Keywords: cognitive sovereignty, AI alignment, data ownership, human judgment, cognitive assets, provenance, AI ethics

1. INTRODUCTION

The question of what artificial intelligence can and cannot replicate from the human mind has been approached primarily as a technical problem. We argue it is, at its core, a property problem.

In early 2026, we conducted a personal experiment with a clear practical motivation: to reclaim time by delegating a portion of professional decision-making to an autonomous agent. The agent was trained using years of interactions with large language model systems, professional email correspondence spanning multiple years, and a structured series of prompts specifically designed to extract the author's cognitive patterns — how risk is evaluated, how options are weighted, how uncertainty is navigated.

The experiment was implemented on Google's Gemini Studio platform over approximately two weeks. The resulting agent, documented at github.com/aalusan/Clonia and clonia.vercel.app, demonstrated a striking asymmetry: in replicating what decisions were made, it performed with notable precision. In replicating who makes them — the specific texture of doubt, the learned tolerance for particular kinds of risk, the intuitions built through years of consequential error — it failed consistently.

The agent sounded like a bot. Because it was one.

This asymmetry between the replicable what and the irreplicable who has antecedents in several philosophical traditions. Ryle's (1949) distinction between knowing-that and knowing-how identifies a category of knowledge that resists propositional capture. Polanyi's (1966) concept of tacit knowledge — 'we can know more than we can tell' — locates the irreducible element precisely where our experiment found it: in the dimension of judgment that cannot be fully articulated, yet reliably guides action. More recently, enactivist approaches to cognition (Varela, Thompson & Rosch, 1991) have argued that knowing is inseparable from the embodied history of the knower — a position our experimental results independently corroborate. We do not claim that this irreducibility is permanent or metaphysically necessary. We claim that it is currently real, that it has economic consequences, and that those consequences create obligations.

2. RELATED WORK

The question of data ownership in AI systems has received increasing attention following high-profile litigation around training data provenance [1, 2, 3]. However, the existing discourse has focused almost exclusively on content ownership — the rights of creators whose text, images, or code appear in training datasets — rather than on the ownership of the reasoning processes that produced that content.

Several platforms have emerged to address adjacent problems. Obsidian [10] and Notion [6] offer personal knowledge management systems that allow users to capture and organize their thinking over time. Obsidian's local-first architecture explicitly positions data ownership as a core value. However, neither platform addresses the economic question of cognitive asset valuation or the legal question of compensation when those assets are used to improve commercial AI systems.

Ekai Labs (2024) introduced the concept of 'cognitive tenants' and described AI training as 'the largest transfer of intelligence in history, one prompt at a time.' Their technical approach focuses on portability and vendor lock-in prevention. Our framework differs in scope: we argue that portability is necessary but insufficient, and that the foundational question is one of property rights and compensation, not merely data custody.

The alignment literature has explored questions of value specification and human oversight [4], but has not substantively addressed the economic and legal dimensions of cognitive asset extraction. Legal scholarship on AI and intellectual property has similarly focused on output ownership rather than input ownership [5].

To our knowledge, no prior work has proposed a comprehensive framework for cognitive sovereignty that integrates the technical, legal, and economic dimensions of human judgment ownership.

2.1 Regulatory Frameworks: Global Gaps in Cognitive Asset Protection

The existing regulatory landscape reveals a systematic asymmetry. While numerous frameworks protect personal data processing and privacy, none establish ownership of or compensation for the cognitive patterns themselves.

2.2 Ley 21.383 and Constitutional Neuroderechos: Chile's Unique Position

On October 25, 2021, Chile reformed Article 19.1 of its Constitution to include explicit protection for 'brain activity' and information derived from it. This reform — promoted by Senator Guido Girardi in collaboration with neurorights experts including Rafael Yuste (Columbia University) — is the first and only constitutional protection of cognitive integrity in any jurisdiction globally.

The cognitive sovereignty framework proposes extending Ley 21.383's constitutional principle into three new dimensions: (1) From Privacy to Ownership (2) From Integrity to Attribution (3) From Consent to Verification.

3. THE PROBLEM: COGNITIVE EXTRACTION AT SCALE

3.1 The Mechanism: The RLHF Pipeline as Extraction

In this paper, the term "cognitive extraction" refers broadly to the implicit capture of human judgment, reasoning patterns, and decision structures through interaction with AI systems. In many cases, this process occurs as an emergent property of machine learning training dynamics rather than through deliberate institutional design.

Contemporary AI platforms extract human judgment through mechanisms that are technically sophisticated and culturally invisible. The process operates primarily through Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF), first formalized by Christiano et al. (2017) and operationalized at scale by Ouyang et al. (2022).

Extraction occurs across three primary channels: (1) Direct Correction Loops (2) Behavioral Telemetry (3) Fine-tuning Interfaces.

3.2 A Documented Case: Samsung Employees and ChatGPT

In April 2023, engineers at Samsung Electronics discovered that colleagues had been pasting confidential source code and trade secrets into ChatGPT — approximately 1 MB of proprietary data per month over three months [34, 35].

3.3 The Posthumous Dimension

In February 2026, Meta Platforms received approval for a patent describing an AI model trained on a user's activity, capable of simulating that user's behavior when absent — including after death [7].

4. A FRAMEWORK FOR COGNITIVE SOVEREIGNTY

4.1 Core Principle

We define cognitive sovereignty as the principle that an individual's reasoning architecture constitutes a protectable and compensable asset that belongs to its originator.

4.1.1 The Property Argument

Three independent arguments: The Lockean argument (labor), The Hegelian argument (personhood), The pragmatic argument (incentive structure).

4.1.2 Rights and Markets: A Necessary Distinction

The base right is inalienable. The derivative right is alienable under terms the individual defines.

4.2 The Three Components

Cognitive Capture, Cryptographic Certification, Compensation Protocol.

4.3 Technical Primitives

MIA, Data Attribution, Cryptographic Watermarking, Federated Learning.

4.4 The Provenance Problem

Provenance must be established prospectively through registration and certification before training occurs.

4.5 The Consent Boundary

ToS-based consent fails three ethical tests: Understanding, Alternatives, Proportional Value Exchange.

5. THE NON-NEGOTIABLE PRINCIPLES

First: Using a person's reasoning architecture without consent is a violation of cognitive sovereignty.

Second: Compensation for cognitive asset extraction is an obligation, not charity.

Third: Posthumous use of cognitive assets requires explicit prior consent.
